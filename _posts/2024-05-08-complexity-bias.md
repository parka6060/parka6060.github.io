*Complexity bias* is our tendency to favour complex explanations and solutions over simple ones; it's a phenomenon that affects various aspects of our lives and is a part of the significant issue of culture that I'd enjoy talking about some other day. Complexity bias is why we endlessly debate with each other, whether it's your diet, exercise routine, or everyday decision-making. It's why the internet is filled with course sellers, spiritual gurus, and Andrew Tates; they're trying to commodify our desires for the most advanced, cutting-edge information. And it's why I find myself trying to figure out the best ways to do things, when there's no need for complexity.

**Interestingly, complex systems are inherently more challenging to sustain over time.** Complexity is both hard to maintain and inefficient. Moreover, the mere *perception* of complexity creates aversion; I have lots of first-hand experience with this. When I first decided to start going to the gym, I found myself spending hours online researching the 'best' fitness routines for beginners. I was anxious and self-conscious about appearing inexperienced and worried about wasting time on inefficient exercises. Caught up in analysis paralysis (the little sibling to complexity bias), I ended up spending far more time researching than I did going to the gym. I started making real progress only once I stopped trying to find the most optimal plan.

Here are a couple of examples of complexity bias I've experienced.
1. Social media strategy: Creating a detailed schedule and long-term plans instead of focusing on posting a couple of times a day.
2. Art practice: Creating a routine for how much and what I should be drawing each day, instead of just setting time out to draw everyday.
3. Nutrition: Tracking every calory and trying elaborate diets, instead of just don't eat too much, and eat mostly plants.
4. Writing: Writing using complex sentences and structure, instead of keeping writing lean and readable.
5. Skincare: 15-step routine (ok, not actually, but you know what I mean) instead of cleanser, moisturizer, and sunscreen.
6. Spirituality: Trying to find God instead of cultivating simple daily mindfulness.

...etc... ...etc... ...etc... ...etc...

The consequences of this bias extend far beyond personal decision-making. It infiltrates our friendships, our work, and our politics. We are surrounded by jargon-filled explanations that obscure us from experience and understanding. A lot of the answers to questions are more straightforward than you can imagine. The path to redemption is not shrouded in mystery; it is a matter of putting one foot in front of the other.
